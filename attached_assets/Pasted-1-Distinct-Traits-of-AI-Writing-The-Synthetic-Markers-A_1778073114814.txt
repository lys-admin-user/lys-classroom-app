1. Distinct Traits of AI Writing (The "Synthetic" Markers)

AI writing is probabilistic; it predicts the "next most likely word," which often results in a distinct, polished, but hollow feel.

The "Hedge" & Lack of Stance: AI rarely takes a definitive or controversial stand. It often uses phrases like "It is important to remember..." or "On the other hand..." to remain neutral.

Uniform Sentence Length: AI tends to produce sentences of similar length and complexity, creating a rhythmic "drone" that can cause a reader to disengage (the opposite of a "Master Teacher's" dynamic delivery).

Predictable Transitions: You will see a heavy reliance on "Furthermore," "In addition," "Moreover," and "In conclusion." Humans vary their transitions more naturally.

Hallucinated Authority: AI can be "confidentially wrong." It will cite a standard or a fact that sounds correct but is functionally inaccurate (e.g., misquoting a TEKS code).

The "Generic" Problem: AI often defaults to clichés and "corporate-speak." It lacks the specific, "in-the-trenches" anecdotes that a real teacher uses to explain a concept.

2. Writing Traits of the "Master Communicators"

The best human communicators—especially the Author Teachers we want in our Academy—display traits that AI currently cannot replicate.

A. The "Vulnerability" Hook

Human Trait: Great writers share their failures. A Master Teacher might write: "I tried teaching photosynthesis this way last year and it was a total disaster—here’s what I learned."

Why it works: It builds immediate trust and rapport, which is essential for the "Being" pillar of our methodology.

B. "Spiky" Point of Views

Human Trait: Best-in-class communicators have a "spiky" opinion—an unconventional truth they believe in.

Why it works: It cuts through the noise. In LYS, this is our stance that "A-F grading is dead; binary success is the future."

C. Tactical Specificity (The "Doing")

Human Trait: Humans use "sensory" details. Instead of saying "Engage the students," a human writes: "Have the students stand up and find someone wearing the same color shoes to start the discussion."

Why it works: It provides a clear, actionable mental model for the "Doing" phase of a lesson.

D. Rhythmic Variance

Human Trait: Great writers use "short-short-long" sentence structures. They use fragments. For emphasis. They follow a 20-word sentence with a 3-word punch.

Why it works: It mimics human speech and keeps the brain "awake" and engaged during a 30-minute lesson plan.

E. The "Recursive" Loop

Human Trait: Humans can refer back to a small joke or a detail from three paragraphs ago (the "callback").

Why it works: It creates a sense of shared history and "Knowing" within a single piece of content.
