Topic: Establishing purpose and making predictions in text (across genres) using Langston Hughes

Lesson objectives:
After the lesson, students will be able to:
- establish a purpose for reading by beginning to generate questions
- make predictions before, during, and after reading

Essential Questions:
Why is it important to set a purpose for a task?

LYS Methodology
Be (Character/Values/Principles) — Spiritual; Purpose
Know (What resources are available to the student) — Vocational; purpose-driven exercises
Do (Execute with Excellence) — Social; discussion

Instructional Input — Asynchronous (the Synchronous flow follows the same sequence below)

AS: Anticipatory Set (Introduction)
Asynchronous situations may require a video walkthrough; a discussion board may be helpful.
Ask students to think of a time they have had to make a prediction — what to wear based on the weather, predicting how someone's mood will be, predicting how a movie will end. Note the "purpose" of their prediction as well.

M: Modeling (I do)
Pull up any grade-appropriate text. Set your purpose aloud (e.g., "I am going to read this article to learn about dog training and I need to make some predictions about what I will learn and need to understand.").
Generate a question that you have and will want to monitor as you read.
Use the "first lines" graphic organizer. Show students how you make predictions based on the first line only of each paragraph.

GP: Guided Practice (We do)
Repeat the steps from the modeling portion but allow students to work alongside you.
Read over the background information about Langston Hughes — background will be critical for understanding the story.
Provide a copy of "Thank you, M'am" plus the anticipation and CSR prediction sheets.
Explain that students will need to form questions before, during, and after reading, and make predictions that they then correct or confirm after reading.
Work together to establish the purpose for reading. Note: you will not be reading the text during this lesson — you are practicing predicting skills and covering background. Students keep their supplies organized for the duration of this lesson group.

Independent Practice / Homework
Students should:
- Establish a purpose for reading the short story
- Begin thinking of questions they may have about Hughes and the story (for the next lesson)
- Engage in informal discussion about purpose and possible questions/predictions based on what we know so far

Lesson Close
What is the answer to the essential question, and why does it matter to me in terms of:

Spiritual? Setting a purpose in even minor tasks keeps you focused on your greater, overall purpose. If you consciously set a structure and meaning for tasks, it will help solidify your holistic spiritual self.

Vocational? It is fair to assume that most of what you do at work will have a specific purpose. There won't be many times that your job has no end game whatsoever. By taking steps now to recognize and establish purpose-driven exercises, you are preparing yourself for this in the real world.

Social? During this lesson — either verbally or via discussion board — you participated in cooperative discussions with your classmates in order to set a purpose and discuss needed information. This cooperation is a crucial social component.
